### F1: Ericsson et al.'s (1993) original framework defines "deliberate practice" narrowly — a teacher-designed activity, distinct from work and play, requiring immediate feedback and repeated corrective attempts — and rests on an explicit, testable "monotonic benefits" assumption
- Provenance: PUBLISHED
- Access: Full PDF directly downloaded and read (pp. 363–370).
- Proposed status bucket: Evidence-backed
- Proposed evidence-quality tier: Q4 (the theoretical-framework/definitional portion of the paper — a
  normative synthesis, not itself an experiment)
- Source(s): Ericsson, K.A., Krampe, R.Th., & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993). "The Role of Deliberate Practice
  in the Acquisition of Expert Performance." *Psychological Review*, 100(3), 363–406.
  DOI: 10.1037/0033-295X.100.3.363
- Proposed supporting location: p. 367 ("Characteristics of Deliberate Practice"); p. 368
  ("Comparison of Deliberate Practice to Other Types of Domain-Related Activities" and "Theoretical
  Framework")
- Claimed strength (exact quotes): "The subjects should receive immediate informative feedback and
  knowledge of results of their performance. The subjects should repeatedly perform the same or
  similar tasks... subjects ideally should be given explicit instructions about the best method and
  supervised by a teacher to allow individualized diagnosis of errors, informative feedback, and
  remedial part training" (p. 367). "The teacher designs practice activities that the individual can
  engage in between meetings with the teacher. We call these practice activities *deliberate
  practice* and distinguish them from other activities, such as playful interaction, paid work, and
  observation of others, that individuals can pursue in the domain" (p. 368).
  **[C3-EC, 2026-07-20 — quote restored to verbatim.]** As originally printed, this "exact quote"
  read "...such as **work, play, and observation of others**" — a paraphrase, not the source's
  wording, apparently back-contaminated from the adjacent p. 368 sentence "Consider three general
  types of activities, namely, work, play, and deliberate practice." Substance is unaffected; the
  quotation marks were the defect. Ground: V3's independent PDF read of EKT 1993 p. 368 (not
  re-read by this pass). "We claim that deliberate practice requires effort and is not inherently
  enjoyable" (p. 368). The "monotonic benefits assumption" — "the amount of time an individual is
  engaged in deliberate practice activities is monotonically related to that individual's acquired
  performance. ... This assumption can be tested empirically" (p. 368).
- Caveats: The definition is bound to a specific delivery structure (individualized teacher + between-
  session assignment); it is this narrow structure, not "practice" generically, that later became the
  contested term (see F3/F7). The paper explicitly frames its own claim as falsifiable.

- Access: Full PDF directly downloaded and read (all 19 pages, incl. Tables 2–5, Figs. 1–5).
- Proposed status bucket: Evidence-backed

  better-executed study of the **same** design class, not a stronger design class. Ground: V3.

  moderator in a random-effects meta-analysis, not a study of blackjack or any stochastic solo-decision

  operationalized from tasks like handling an aviation emergency. Mapping blackjack onto this tier —

  differences is smaller, not zero or negative, when the task environment is less predictable," not
  "practice doesn't work in stochastic domains." Overstating this in the causal direction would be the
  same failure mode as the original dossier's omission, just pointed the other way. (3) **[C3-EC —
  REWRITTEN; the original account of this gap was wrong in a way that matters for how the product
  reads the finding.]** **Predictability was modelled as a *continuous meta-regression moderator*,
  not as three subgroups — so the low/moderate/high percentages are *fitted values read off a
  regression line*, not subgroup means, and there is no per-tier k because there are no tiers.** The
  evidence for this is structural and convergent: the test is **Q(1) with a slope b = 0.14** (one
  degree of freedom — a regression slope), where domain, a genuine categorical moderator, gets
  **Q(4)**; the 2014 main text lists predictability as **the quantitative variable** when reporting
  interrater agreement ("Cohen's kappa for the *categorical* variables and Spearman's rho for the
  *quantitative variable*... predictability of the task environment: ρs = .89–.96", p. 1611); the

  moderators and reports no predictability rows at all** — confirmed by this pass's direct read;
  and Figure S1 is described in the Supplement as "a **scatterplot**... **The positive slope**
  indicates..." — a regression, not tier means.
  *As originally printed, caveat (3) said no per-tier k "is reported" and that "Figure S1 and the
  meta-analysis's Open Data file... would be needed to check it," framing the missing k as a
  **retrievable** gap and logging it as a future lead. That is wrong: the per-tier k is

  **What this changes for the product — this is the load-bearing consequence.** There are **no bands
  to place blackjack into; there is a slope.** Mapping blackjack onto "the low-predictability tier"

**But the enlarged dossier as a whole still fails the "corrected figures presented as authoritative"

**3. The strength of the mapping — the characterisation is CONFIRMED, and the dossier's framing is
honest; but the moderator's *method* is misdescribed in a way that matters.**

The "low predictability = handling an aviation emergency, not a card game" characterisation is
accurate and verbatim-sourced (p. 1609). The dossier's two qualifications — (a) analogy not
measurement, (b) a moderator of *variance explained*, not evidence that practice fails — are both
correct, both necessary, and are, in my judgement, the most disciplined writing anywhere in this
dossier. It explicitly warns against overreach *in both directions*, which is the right instinct for a
number this product-consequential. I find no overreach in either direction. **Strength-honest: passes.**

**However, F11's caveat (3) misdescribes how the moderator was modelled, and thereby mis-states what is
missing and how retrievable it is.** The dossier says "No per-tier sample size (k) is reported… so the
low-predictability tier's precision cannot be assessed… Figure S1 and the meta-analysis's Open Data
file (not obtained in this pass) would be needed to check it." That frames the missing k as a
*retrievable* gap. It is not, and the reason is structural:

- Predictability was modelled as a **continuous meta-regression moderator, not as three subgroups.**
  The test is **Q(1) with a slope b = 0.14** — one degree of freedom — where domain, a genuine
  categorical moderator, gets Q(4). One df is a regression slope, not a three-group contrast.
- The 2014 main text states this explicitly when reporting interrater agreement: "Cohen's kappa for
  the **categorical** variables and Spearman's rho for the **quantitative variable**… predictability of
  the task environment: ρs = .89–.96" (p. 1611). Predictability *is* the quantitative variable.
- The predictability score is the average of two 1–3 ratings, so it takes five values (1, 1.5, 2, 2.5,
  3), not three.
- **Table 1 (p. 1612) reports k and N for domain and for both methodological moderators, and reports
  no predictability rows at all** — consistent with there being no subgroups to count.
- Figure S1 is described in the Supplement as "a **scatterplot** representing the relationship between
  predictability… and the Fisher's Z transformation of the effect size… **The positive slope**
  indicates…" — a regression, not tier means.

So the 24%/12%/4% (and 23%/14%/6%) figures are **fitted values read off a regression line at low,
moderate and high points of a continuous predictor**, not the means of three tiers of studies. There is
no per-tier k because there are no tiers. **This does not weaken the finding and I am not downgrading
its bucket** — but it changes what a downstream reader should do with it in two ways worth stating:
(i) chasing Figure S1 or the Open Data file for a per-tier k, as the dossier's "future lead" suggests,
would be wasted effort; (ii) mapping blackjack onto "the low tier" is not category assignment but
**assigning blackjack a predictability score on two 1–3 scales and reading the fitted value** — which
makes the dossier's "analogy, not direct measurement" warning *more* apt, not less, and makes the
precision of the low end depend on how many effect sizes actually sit near the low end of the observed
range (unknown, and genuinely unavailable from the published article + corrigendum + supplement).

**Answer to the card's question:** the per-tier k is **genuinely unavailable, not merely unlocated** —
and the dossier's stated reason for its absence is wrong.

## A version hazard this card exposed (recorded because it nearly produced a false kill — by me)

My first independent copy of F8 was the **LSE Research Online accepted manuscript**. Against it, the
dossier's F8 quote looked like a reconstruction: the accepted version reads "Ericsson (2014-this issue)
mounts a vigorous defense of his view—but one we believe fails for **four major reasons**" where the
dossier has "…is, in our view, unsuccessful for **several reasons**"; the concluding paragraph is
differently worded; and the internal page cite is "(p. 4)" where the dossier has "(p. 84)". On that
evidence I had provisionally written F8 down to "substance verified, wording not verbatim."

**That would have been a false finding.** The **published** *Intelligence* version — obtained on
re-check from the URL recorded in source-lead register entry #14 — matches the dossier's quote **word
for word, including "(p. 84)"**. The accepted manuscript and the published article differ
substantially, well beyond copyediting; the "(p. 4)" → "(p. 84)" shift is the tell, because the
published reply cites Ericsson (2014) at its journal pagination (81–103) while the manuscript cites its
own preprint pages.

Two consequences worth carrying forward:
- **The collector read the published version.** The "(p. 84)" cite is affirmative evidence of that — a
  reconstruction from an accepted manuscript could not have produced it.
- **Source-lead register entry #14 points at the LSE accepted copy** and records that V3 "located and
  partially read" the reply *via that copy*. Anyone verifying against #14's route will hit the same
  divergence I did and may reach the same wrong conclusion. Appended as lead #28 so the next pass
  doesn't repeat it.

This is the same class of hazard the card warned me about — not a quote whose *voice* was misassigned,
but a quote whose *edition* was. I checked voice-attribution explicitly on both F8 and F9 (the two
counter-replies, i.e. exactly the papers where each camp quotes the other at length) and found **no
sign-flips**: every quoted Ericsson-side phrase in both findings is correctly presented as an
opponent's words being rebutted, not as a concession.

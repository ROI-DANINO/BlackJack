1. **What important evidence appears missing**
   - **The applied-behaviour-analysis (ABA) / behavioral-skills-training tradition on gambling.** Not one of the four literatures the dossier searched, and it is the single tradition that actually runs *training interventions on blackjack*. Named, locatable sources:
     - **Speelman, R.C., Whiting, S.W. & Dixon, M.R. (2015). "Using behavioral skills training and video rehearsal to teach blackjack skills." *Journal of Applied Behavior Analysis*, 48(3), 632–642. DOI 10.1002/jaba.225 (PubMed 26173744; ERIC EJ1073394).** Multiple-baseline design; 4 recreational gamblers taught card counting via video instruction/rehearsal/testing; **no participant counted accurately at baseline**; all reached **100% accuracy under changing-speed-criterion training**; generalisation probes conducted while playing blackjack in a **mock casino**; 3 of 4 won substantially after the intervention having lost during baseline.
     - **"Using Behavioral Skills Training to Improve Casino Blackjack Strategy: Replication and Extension"** — a direct replication+extension of the above.
     - **Speelman, R.C.,** doctoral dissertation, *"Using behavioral skills training to promote optimal blackjack strategies"*, Southern Illinois University (opensiuc.lib.siu.edu/dissertations/1265/).
     - Surrounding literature: *Analysis of Gambling Behavior* (e.g. Dixon & Jackson 2008, performance feedback to teach video-poker players); review: *"Trends in Behavior-Analytic Gambling Research and Treatment"*, PMC4883474.
   - **Anderson, G. & Brown, R.I.F. (1984). "Real and laboratory gambling, sensation-seeking and arousal." *British Journal of Psychology*, 75, 401–410. DOI 10.1111/j.2044-8295.1984.tb01910.x — 466 citations.** Subjects played **blackjack** in both a real casino and a laboratory; significant differences between conditions in heart rate, gambling behaviour, and the arousal/sensation-seeking relationships; the paper explicitly casts "doubt on laboratory gambling as a valid analogue of the real gambling situation."

2. **Why the omission could materially affect the findings** — it reverses three of the dossier's four headline conclusions, and the two most product-relevant ones.
   - **Q1 coverage gap ("No controlled instructional/training-intervention study was found… This is a genuine absence, not a search failure") — falsified.** Speelman et al. is exactly that study: a controlled training intervention with baseline, staged protocol, fluency criterion, and post-training performance measurement.
   - **Q2 coverage gap ("No peer-reviewed study… measures card-counting acquisition, the working-memory load… or counting accuracy under realistic table speed") — falsified.** Speelman et al. trains card counting specifically and measures accuracy under a **changing speed criterion**, which is the speed-load question the dossier declares untested. The dossier's dismissal of counting-training claims as "Q6 self-report/marketing claims about practitioners' own methods" is therefore wrong in kind, not just in degree — there is peer-reviewed Q2-tier evidence that trained counting transfers to play.

That is a live **opposing-positions** structure on the transfer question, and the dossier represents neither side — it substitutes a single inferential bridge (F6) from a different game entirely.

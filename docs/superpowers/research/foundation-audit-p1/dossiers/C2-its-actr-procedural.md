### F4: ACT theory (Anderson, 1982) proposes a ~~three-stage~~ **two-stage** account of skill acquisition **[corrected by C2-EC, per V2]** — a declarative stage (facts interpreted by general-purpose procedures, verbally mediated, slow, high working-memory load), a knowledge-compilation transition (via the subprocesses of *composition*, which collapses production sequences into one production, and *proceduralization*, which embeds facts directly into productions), and a procedural stage (fast, seamless, no verbal mediation)
- Provenance: PUBLISHED
- Access: Full PDF directly read (pp. 369–374 read in full: abstract, "The ACT Production System," "An Example," "Learning in ACT," start of "The Declarative Stage").
- Proposed status bucket: Evidence-backed
- Proposed evidence-quality tier: Q4 (theoretical synthesis instantiated in a running production-system simulation, illustrated with small worked examples such as column-addition and geometry proofs, not a large randomized study)
- Source(s): Anderson, J.R. (1982). "Acquisition of Cognitive Skill." *Psychological Review*, 89(4), 369–406. https://gwern.net/doc/iq/1982-anderson-2.pdf
- Proposed supporting location: Abstract; pp. 369–370 ("The Declarative Stage").
- Claimed strength (exact quote): "A framework for skill acquisition is proposed that includes two major stages in the development of a cognitive skill: a declarative stage in which facts about the skill domain are interpreted and a procedural stage in which the domain knowledge is directly embodied in procedures for performing the skill... Knowledge compilation is the process by which the skill transits from the declarative stage to the procedural stage. It consists of the subprocesses of composition, which collapses sequences of productions into single productions, and proceduralization, which embeds factual knowledge into productions."
- **Correction to this finding's framing [C2-EC, per V2]:** the original headline said "three-stage." Anderson's abstract — quoted correctly in this very finding — says "**two major stages**." - Caveats: This is a theoretical framework instantiated in a symbolic production-system model and illustrated with worked examples (addition, geometry, LISP programming), not validated against a large empirical dataset in this 1982 paper itself — record as theoretical assertion with illustrative, not statistical, support. Anderson explicitly states elsewhere in the paper (p.369) that "it requires at least 100 hours of learning and practice to acquire any significant cognitive skill to a reasonable degree of proficiency" — a claim presented as a framing observation, not as something this paper itself measures.
